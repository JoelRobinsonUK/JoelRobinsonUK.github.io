Play on itch.io

Monster Factory is a game about a down on his luck villain who discovers a disused castle. In a bid to make a comeback the player will turn the castle into a fully operational Monster Factory, and create minions of various functions and forms for the other villains of the land.The player will build and upgrade equipment, complete contracts by designing and producing monsters, and raid the area around their castle for materials and funds.

As the gameplay isn't explained by an in game tutorial I made this short video walkthrough.

  • Context

    The initial inspiration for Monster Factory was the Polygon video series of the same name in which Justin and Griffin McElroy experiment with the character customisation options of various games to create strange results.In particular the 3 part series in which they explored the game Spore served to inspire the idea of designing your own monsters instead of them being predefined creatures. Spore itself inspired some of the mechanics behind the monster design mechanics, though ours is considerably simplified from what Maxis designed.

    For this game we looked at games such as the Overlord series by Triumph Studios, the Dungeon Keeper game and a bit of Overcooked. Overlord and Dungeon keeper tie into the villain aspect of our game and how we don’t play as a conventional, morally white protagonist but rather the antagonist of such stories. The idea of building minions for other villains is similar to how Overlord allows you to manage your own minions and build up a small army of them to control. The inspiration from Dungeon Keeper comes from the game being centered around building specific rooms to complete a dungeon, much like our game has the player build certain machines to create their monsters.


  • Mechanics

    Factory Upgrades
    One of the core gameplay elements in Monster Factory is the expansion of the factory’s collection of machines. In the current version of the game the player can only utilise one set of ma-chines: one Sorting Machine, a Paint Cauldron, an Armourer, and a Reanimator, and as many conveyor belts in between as they choose.Because the location from which monsters spawn to be assem-bled is static the player has to set up these machines in one location, and there is no way to fork them off onto a section production line. The latter issue could be resolved by introducing a “splitter” variant of the conveyor belt, which alternates sending the monster bodies in different directions.Despite the limitation of their options the player is able to move, rotate, and delete machines from the scene at any time. In future versions it would be beneficial for the player to recover some re-sources when destroying their machines, this was not implement-ed now because it would require significant redesigning of how both machine deletion and resource management are coded.

    Resource Management
    The game is heavily dependent on the resource system for the creation of machines and monsters. It is also integral to the raiding mechanic, where resources are gained. Presently raiding is the only way to get resources, however it would be better if future iterations of the game also allowed the player to directly purchase these resources as well. This is another case of the way resources are structured getting in the way of a good idea.The player can view their current amount of resources in the Resources tab, as well as their total available space. Storage is de-termined by how many crates the player has, each crate provides 100 slots for each resource.Gold is also considered a resource, though it has a fixed cap of 1 million, and two other major resources: electricity and health (i.e. the integrity of the factory) were planned but were not prioritised for development.

    Contracts
    The player’s objective in the game is the completion of monsters for clients. The contract lists key information to the player such as how many monsters are needed, what size they should be and what attributes they should have. In the current version of the game only two contracts exist, as the game release is intended only as a demo, it would realistically be very simple to randomly generate contracts and ensure the player has a number to choose from at all times.When the player completes the number of monsters required for the contract the monsters’ attributes and size are compared against those listed in the contract, and a score is assigned based on the percentage of matched criteria. The same percentage of the reward money is given to the player.

    Monster Creation
    When the player accepts a contract they are presented with the monster creation page. Here they are given the option of assem-bling monster parts from three archetype monsters: a fishman, a zombie, and a ghoul. They can also give the monster a weapon. Each body part and weapon comes with a number of attributes, these are shown on the right side of the screen to allow the player to make sure they make the right monster. The player can also choose to make the monster large, medium, or small; and can give it a custom colour and name. The latter options are purely for the player’s amusement, though the thought behind giving the monster a name comes from the future possibility of saving the player’s creations for easy reproduction down the line. At all times the player is able to see how much the monster will cost them in resources on the right side of the screen, if the requirements exceed capacity they will be unable to create the monster. At the moment the player cannot access the monster creation menu without accepting a contract, so they would have to cancel the contract and start over if they do not create enough monsters the first time.


  • Reflection

    I feel this is the most complex game I’ve worked on to date in terms of programming and it’s definitely my most untidy. A lot of my code was written as isolated mechanics which I then had to mash together to turn into the game you see. I think a project of this size and ambition needed a longer period of concept design, so we could lock down our priorities better and also plan out how things would work. In particular I feel my development of the resource system was rushed and very short-sighted, for such an integral part of the game’s structure it is far too difficult to interact with in other scripts. But I know now how I could do a dozen things better than I have done on this occasion and I feel good about the knowledge I’m taking away.I’m also very proud of the team and what we’ve accomplished together. Ash’s art and Jack’s level design really give the game its own personality, and George’s contributions to the code are invaluable to the gameplay. I think we could have worked better as a team generally, we were quite disorganised and we didn’t keep ourselves to a rigid development timeline. More stringent following of an Scrum system would have helped. And at times I think the fact that we are such good friends led to difficulty in being honest with one another when we felt the work being done wasn’t up to standard. As George says I feel keeping friends and colleagues separate is the way to go.In all I think the Monster Factory we’ve released is a fantastic demonstration of what the game could become with dedicated development time when there isn’t a global pandemic happening.


Play on itch.io

Calling is an endless runner incorporating core mechanics of Tabletop RPGs. The player chooses a class and rolls dice to determine their Strength, Constitution, Dexterity, and Luck. These stats govern different parts of the endless running, where the player must attack and jump to survive. Both of these actions, as well as everything from enemy spawn rates to loot drops, are determined by the players’ stat modifiers.

Screenshots from the released game

  • Context

    One of the most popular categories of web games is the endless runner. These games focus on mechanics relating to dodging obstacles that approach the player, usually from the right side of the screen as the player runs left. These obstacles are almost always procedurally generated.

    My prototype project would be an endless runner designed around the gameplay fundamentals of tabletop role playing games like Dungeons and Dragons.

    When the player starts the game they will be prompted to roll a twenty sided die (a D20), four times. The player would then assign their rolled numbers to four core stats: strength, dexterity, constitution, and luck. Whenever the player takes an action during a round of the game it will be affected by these stats. Modifiers are determined in a bracket fashion, as shown in this table.


  • Mechanics

    Loading and Character Creation
    When the game starts it checks for locally saved data. If it finds data for any of the three character slots the data will be displayed, and clicking on that slot loads the character (more on that later). If no information is found the player is presented with empty slots, clicking on one of these leads to the character creation screens (and sets the “active slot variable accordingly). First the player chooses a class, for which a string variable is assigned when they make their choice. This string is easily one of the most important variables in the game, for reasons I’ll get into soon.

    Following class selection the player rolls their stat dice. When the center button is clicked the four dice on the left side of the screen display a number between 1 and 20. This number is saved to an instance variable on each dice object, so that when the player drags a dice into one of the four slots on the right of the screen the displayed number is assigned to the corresponding stat. There is an issue on this screen that if the player drops one of their dice on top of another they lose the ability to separate the two dice. This is an issue I thought I had fixed early in development when I first found it, but it has returned in the current build.

    Saving
    Once the player has rolled their stats they are taken to the character review screen. They must name their character and then click save, or go back to make last minute changes to their character. When the player saves their character the UI of the character screen changes forever, the “are you happy” text is deleted, the displays for gold, level, xp, and record distance appear; along with a delete button.

    After these UI changes the game creates a save slot using the “active slot” variable. This save slot stores all the static information that will never change about the character, such as name, class and base stats; without me having to individually save each piece of information. That said, some stats are saved to the local storage as well using the naming scheme of “C + active slot + _NameOfVariable”. Some of this information, like name and class, are saved so they can be shown when the game searches for character information when it loads, the rest is saved for convenient comparison and updating.

    Actions
    When the player flicks up to jump a number is generated between 1 and 20, and their dexterity modifier is added to that number. This roll is compared against a few ranges (1 to 7, 8 to 11, and 12 to 20), if the player’s roll is in the low bracket they have “failed” in their action and will not jump very high, the middle bracket gives moderate jump height, and the upper bracket gives a good jump height. If the initial number before the dexterity modifier is added is a 1 then the player will not jump at all and they will be slowed down for 10 seconds (this effect is achieved by halving the environment speed and score speed variables for that time). If the initial number is a 20 then they would receive a reward (at this time the game does not seem to give 20s).

    Pick Ups
    Every 30 seconds to a minute a pick up object will spawn and move across the screen, currently it is impossible for the player to miss these, but in later versions I would like to add some challenge.

    The first pick up is a health potion, which adds 3 points of health back to the player, or if they are less than 3 points from their maximum health (max health is 10 + constitution mod) then it restores them to full so as to cause no overflow.

    Next is a luck potion, this briefly adds 5 points to the player’s luck mod, and any events affected by the mod in this time will benefit.

    The speed potion multiplies the environment speed and score speed by 1.5x for 10 seconds, giving the impression that the character had a sudden burst of speed.

    The coin pouch gives a random amount of money between 10 and 50 multiplied by the luck mod (unless luck mod is negative). The book gives an amount of XP between 5 and 25, no modifier.

    Game End
    When the player runs out of health a lot happens. First their score is compared against the leaderboard database to determine their ranking, once the game has access to that number the gameplay layer of the game layout (which has the character, enemies and UI on it) is disabled and the post game layer is shown.

    The player’s rank is displayed at the top to encourage them to play again and get higher, then their gold and XP acquisitions are shown below. If the player’s XP gains (once added to their current XP) are higher than the current level target value for that character then their level increases by one, and their XP is set to anything that exceeds the target. A text element becomes visible to indicate they’ve leveled up. Lastly the player’s distance is shown. If it’s better than their current record they are shown a banner congratulating them, and their new highscore is saved.

    The player has a few options of how to proceed, they can share their score to Twitter (with different text if they break their record), play again immediately, or return to the character screen. Either of the latter options will first save the player’s score to the leaderboard database for ranking. I had intended for this to only happen when the player exceeds their high score, however when I tried this it would submit the score dozens of times.


  • Reflection

    Calling: A Side Roller is much less polish than I had hoped it would be. I feel this is a result of my lack of experience with Construct 3 as well as some of the quirks that the engine has. As it currently exists the game is finicky to a degree I feel makes it unenjoyable to play. However in the process of getting feedback several sources expressed their interest in the concept itself. One said the game was “a nice little itch scratched” in terms of its ‘Dungeons and Dragons Lite’ style of gameplay. This is exactly the aim of my original concept, and shows that there is potential in Calling.

    I’m disappointed that I had to cut some content from the released game, namely the quest achievements and the gear store. I feel that with these mechanics the game would have been significantly more robust and deliver better on its promise of a simple TTRPG experience. I would be interested in continuing development of Calling: A Side Roller in my own time in order to introduce these mechanics, and in order to prepare the game fully as a Facebook instant game, which I feel is the place where the game has the best chance of success.

Geoff Bozo Simulator a game where the player makes decisions as a number of billionaire characters, based on real life individuals. Each character starts with a base net worth and salary, and social standing. In each of ten rounds the player is presented with a scenario and two options of how to resolve it. The decisions made by the player affect the billionaire’s wealth and social standing. At the end of the game the player is presented with a review of their decisions and then a brief epilogue.

Here's a quick video preview showing one of the outcomes in the game.

  • Context

    This game responds to the subject of the moral and financial decisions of billionaires in society, in particular regarding their contributions to major issues such as combating climate change, world hunger. The game also addresses their treatment of workers and their less than honest approaches to paying taxes.


  • Learning Outcome

    The core objective of the game is to show the target audience, working and middle class people who think billionaires are right to keep their money to themselves, that individuals with that kind of money could contribute to many important causes and still remain the richest people on Earth. Further, the game aims to teach players that wealth creates wealth, and as a result the rich will continue getting richer by making decisions that negatively impact the other 99% of society.


  • Mechanics

    Geoff Bozo Simulator begins by giving the player the choice of five billionaires to play as, each based on a member of the top ten richest people in the United States. After the player has chosen their character they are presented with the main screen. They can see their character’s company, salary, and current bank value (accurate to 2019), as well as their social standing (how the general populace feel about them).

    The player can also see a description of a scenario, and two buttons that show their options. The player has 60 seconds to decide on a solution to the scenario, though they can also choose to skip the round.

    After the first round the player will begin to see a news ticker at the bottom of the screen detailing the outcome of their decision. As the player makes their decisions they will see how much money they are making in the transaction history box. Each choice will change their salary, and have an immediate cost or gain. Salary is paid into the player’s bank at the end of each round.


  • Reflection

    I feel that this game achieves it aims well, the message is for the most part clear to the player and the information imparted to them through it is accurate and compelling toward the point being made. I feel that there is room for the message to be made for concrete while also making its delivery more subtle. I feel at times that the game too obviously shows my personal distaste for billionaires.

    The development of this game has greatly improved my understanding of Unity’s UI system, and I look forward to applying this knowledge to future projects. I have also improved my general development habits through my use of GitHub for this project, and have been more successful identifying issues and resolving them.

    I feel that in future this game could be improved by taking more time to go through each scenario, and provide greater detail to the player. Perhaps the main screen could focus on that while information such as the bank and transaction history are tidied away into a side menu. The game would also benefit from being rewritten with more neutral language, this would improve the learning outcomes by not leading the player. I also think that the end of game descriptions are not diverse enough, I would like to make them more dependent on a number of factors rather than how many of each choice the player made, as it is the social value has little function.

The VRcade is a prototype VR arcade hub developed as a part of my dissertation exploring the question:
"How can virtual reality technology be used to revive the arcade experience for a contemporary audience?"
My aim was to design a diagetic game library that could serve as the access point for the games available in a brick and mortar VR arcade, and also work as a virtual social space. I concluded my report with the findings that while virtual reality would be a hugely beneficial partner to the ailing arcade industry, the opposite is also true: arcades could be the key to VR gaming becoming a mainstream platform.

Trouble’s Brewing is a VR game about preparing potions to fulfil orders as quickly as possible. In the game the player must experiment to discover the effects of ingredients at their disposal and practice their technique at chopping, stirring, and boiling to produce the highest quality potions. Throughout the game the player will have the opportunity to upgrade their equipment and explore the story of the town they work in.

We presented a working prototype at the Bristol VR Lab in June 2019, and although we received positive feedback I feel that there is so much room to improve this project.

This demo shows the basic mechanics of the game, which make up the process of making a potion.

  • Role

    For this project I agreed to be the scrum master for development. While we did not commit to a structured Agile development cycle, I did make the effort to set a few specific goalposts. In particular I aimed for us to have enough of our planned mechanics in the prototype that the player would be able to make a potion from scratch. I designed the game’s logo, using the Potion Fushia font and incorporating a few elements of the theme into the title. I was also responsible for preparing the Game Design Document for presentation at the showcase.

    In terms of development I was responsible for creating original 3D assets for the game, as well a programming mechanics. I would say that I had the most input on what our prototype ended up being like, though development was more often than not democratic.


  • Context

    The primary inspiration for the game is Overcooked. The idea of the pace becoming more frantic as time goes on was central to the original concept we developed, though as we began to design the game the feature was put on the list of least important features. One of the features of Overcooked that remained in our concept throughout was the need for players to manage multiple tasks at the same time. Though this isn’t visible in the prototype the addition of more cauldrons and more than one quest being active at any given time introduces this challenge in Trouble’s Brewing.

    We also took inspiration from Skyrim. While our aesthetic is much more whimsical and lighter our fantasy setting is in part informed by Skyrim’s. We borrowed the concept of ingredients having more than one function from Skyrim’s potion making mechanics and adjusted it to our own concept of ingredients working differently depending on which of the four kinds of brew the player is trying to make.


  • Mechanics

    We started development with plans for six mechanics just within the potion brewing pipeline: chopping, grinding, filtering, stirring, boiling and bottling. Filtering was abandoned as soon as I was unable to make a pleasing model of an alembic, and we decided against bottling as having a fully formed bottle of potion appear above the cauldron better suited the style of the game.

    While stirring and boiling were properly implemented, along with the ability to add ingredients to the cauldron, we were unable to find a way to introduce chopping and grinding to the prototype, and the boiling mechanic is more forgiving that the one we had planned. Therefore, in the prototype the player faces no penalties for poor technique or for not timing the boiling properly, both elements that would have factored into a quality rating system we considered as part of the “to do if we have time” list.

    There were two key mechanics that we were unable to get working in time to be part of the prototype. The first was the ability to remove ingredients from the jars that populate the environment, parts of this feature were set up, but the scripts that handled it were not completed. The second, and more significant in my opinion, is the lack of names on the potions that the player produces.


  • Reflection

    Overall, I am very satisfied with the state of Trouble’s Brewing as it currently stands. The visual appeal of the game is very strong, I think that the combination of low poly models and the toon shader effect creates a fun environment so removed from reality that there is no risk of uncanny valley discomfort. The way the included mechanics feel and the way they interact with George’s sound work confirm that this game works better in VR that it possibly could on a conventional platform.

    The few issues that the current build has do not impact the player experience as much as I expected they would, however I feel that the inclusion of the missing ingredient interaction, chopping, and grinding would greatly elevate the gameplay. I also think that replacing the existing guide text, which was only included for the sake of the showcase, with a diegetic guide book for the player to read through, as well as a UI element over the remotes that show the name of what is being hovered over or held would leave the game feeling much more polished.

    I would like to continue working on this project, as I feel the concept has a lot of potential, and with more time we as a team could turn it into a game worth publishing.

In this industry-based assessment, I worked as part of team to deliver a prototype mobile game to the games team at Aardman Animations. I was heavily involved with developing the early concept, as well as scripting the UI and hooking it into the game mechanics. This prototype was presented at a showcase at the Bristol VR Lab where it was well received by Aardman.
Aardman tasked us with developing a casual game promoting the upcoming series of Shaun the Sheep. Our concept for this was a farm themed tower defense game for mobile and tablet platforms, featuring the characters of the show facing off against their frequent antagonists: the pigs.

A few screenshots from the prototype we presented at the Bristol VR Lab.

  • Role

    In the run up to our pitching session at Aardman I was responsibe for researching the mechanics of popular tower defense games and picking out the ones that would fit well with our theme and target audience.
    Following this when developing the prototype I designed and scripted the UI system for the player to spawn sheep, hide them from the farmer, and move between scenes.
    When the prototype was ready to be built Saul and I worked together to get the iOS build ready, and I also built and Android verison to look for platform specific issues that might be worth investigating.
    I also designed and edited the Game Design Document from scratch in Microsoft Office Publisher.


  • Context

    One of the core inspirations for the feel of our prototype is Bloons Tower Defense an Adobe Flash game based around popping balloons before they reached the end of the path. This mechanic of preventing an object from reaching its destination forms the core of A Porky Predicament’s gameplay, as we intended for strategic placement to be necessary to succeed. We also took inspiration from Bloon’s introduction of more enemies in later levels.

    We also took some cues from Plants vs Zombies, a game in which the enemy characters have a variety of attributes that allow gameplay to remain fresh between levels, and encouraging careful strategy to win. We planned a system similar to Plants vs Zombies’ “Sun” currency, where the player would have limited resources for each level.


  • Mechanics

    The prototype contains most of the core gameplay mechanics we included in our pitch to Aardman. Once the player presses “start” they have 25 seconds seconds to position their sheep assets to bar the path of the pigs, which begin moving up from the barn when the preparation time is over.

    If the player has positioned the sheep poorly then the pigs will reach the farmhouse and alert the farmer to the animals’ human behaviour. If the player places their sheep well the pigs won’t reach the farmhouse in time, however the player must still press the “Bitzer” button to remove their sheep from the field before the farmer comes out and sees.

    In the current build the player has access to four sheep: Shirley, who is large enough to block the path but who can be moved by the pigs after a few second; Timmy, who slows down the pigs by crying loudly; Timmy’s Mother, who lays down a yarn tripwire that knocks pigs out; and Nuts, who is mad enough to charge straight at the pigs and knock them off the screen.


  • Reflection

    Although the game seems very basic and cumbersome I am very satisfied with the progress we made with it as a prototype. I feel that the decision to work on the concept as a grey box prototype allowed us to focus more on getting the essential mechanics in place, though I would have liked to make the game look nicer before showing it off. I think if we had decided to try and produce something closer to a release ready version the art side of the team would have had the opportunity to contribute more to the prototype itself rather than just generating additional concept art on the side.

    The major technical let down we faced with this project is the way the player positions sheep, we originally intended for this to be a grid system with predefined positions on and to either side of the path, however we were unable to get this system working, and the partial version that Sam helped us with became too broken to work with when we tried to extend it, so we rolled back to an earlier project version and started over with the current system. If we were going to continue this project with Aardman I would prioritise getting that mechanic working first, as I feel that the current method of freely dragging objects around negatively impacts how enjoyable the game is in its current form.

    I feel that while we worked well as a team the project could have been managed a little better, with more emphasis on using the agile system and having a clear roadmap, rather than focusing on one problem at a time.


Play on itch.io

This game began as a hasty attempt to hand something in when I lost all the files to my originally planned narrative game. I took a 3D model of my bedroom that I had worked on for another assignment into Unity and added a simple fetch quest. I chose to theme the game around Bethesda's Todd Howard as Fallout 76 had just come out and I had a lot of feelings that needed venting about the whole thing.

This playthrough of the game highlights some of the logarithmic fall-off I experimented with.

  • Context

    This game is an escape room style 3D game where the player must collect the character’s possessions from around the room and leave. The game contains a variety of environmental storytelling elements that rely on niche context and humour.
    This game’s mechanics are inspired by slow paced narrative games like Gone Home and Everybody's Gone to the Rapture. However, the theme of the game is mocking Todd Howard, Bethesda’s Creative Director and a nexus of internet meme activity.
    The environmental assets for this game were created from scratch using Autodesk 3DS Max. Each object was also uniquely textured using Substance Painter. As I had already made these assets, I considered building on the environment to create a large playing space and give the player more to do, however I felt that expanding the area beyond one room did not offer enough opportunity to improve on game mechanics to be worth the time needed to design additional assets.


  • Mechanics

    The scripts for this game are very simple. The Item script is attached to every object the player needs to collect, and checks whether the player is in the object’s collider. When the player is in the collider the script displays a GUI element that prompts the player to pick the object up. When the player presses the appropriate key, the object is destroyed, and the relevant Fungus block is executed. The Game Manager script checks that all the objects have been collected before prompting the player to exit the room with another Fungus block and deleting the Game Manager object.
    The room environment is surrounded by a reverb zone using the “room” preset, this makes the audio elements played inside the zone sound more like they’re coming from an interior space. The music coming from the desk area is set up with a logarithmic rolloff that means it is audible throughout the whole room but gets quieter further from the source. The music playing behind the door uses a linear roll off that gives it a smaller range.


  • Reflection

    I feel that as a piece of satire this game is very successful, however as a narrative experience it doesn’t meet the standard that I aspire to. I think had there been time to recover my lost original project the narrative I had planned for it would have better suited the requirements. I also feel that the room could benefit from more interactivity beyond the essential items. I do think that this game reaches a high level of visual polish, due to all the models and textures being based off real life objects.
