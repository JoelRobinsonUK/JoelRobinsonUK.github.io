Professional Development Plan & SMART Goals

I am currently very comfortable working in Unity and C#, and am becoming increasingly familiar with Javascript and web development. I’ve also had some experience with 3D modelling and feel that I am reasonably well acquainted with most aspects of asset design. I feel my real strength lies in the game concepts I’ve produced, both those that I’ve created prototypes of and those that I’m still ruminating on.

In the longest term my goal is to reach the role of lead game designer or producer at a small or medium scale studio (nothing AAA) and I already have a game idea that I think needs to be saved until this time to reach its full potential. Prior to this I would naturally aim to work my way up through the games industry, and while I do have experience with the development and coding side I would prefer to stay on the design side of things if I can.

In the medium term I would like to develop a full, but small, game entirely on my own, to a higher quality than the prototypes I developed during undergrad. I have a few ideas for this which would probably be very achievable. That said, the game I really want to make is a fully realised version of “Trouble’s Brewing”, the VR game I worked on as part of a team in second year. I think that game is the most promising and interesting idea I’ve worked on.

Short term my goal is to continue building on my technical skill set. There are a number of technologies that I feel I need to become better acquainted with before I can put out an entire game on my own. Chief among these is the use of databases, which I only recently started working with. I think I have the basics down but I can definitely get better at implementing them. Ideally I’d like to learn how to use Unreal Engine, as it seems to be a more commonly used tool than Unity in bigger studios and would improve my employability.

I think for the most part completion of this masters course will boost me along the way to achieving my long term goals, however it may further benefit me to get more specific accreditation in some of the key tools of the trade. I am aware that LinkedIn has a system of awarding accreditation to listed skills on completion of tests, but I haven’t been able to determine if those actually have value in the eyes of employers. I should aim to reinforce my portfolio considerably, cutting it down to a few more impressive projects rather than showing my entire history of work. Further to this I should make more effort to push my portfolio out there, and establish a more noticeable online presence generally. I think working on a long term development project would help with this, as it would naturally generate content that I can share as I progress. I’ve seen this used successfully to build online presence and to build hype for indie games before, so I see no reason why it wouldn’t work for me.

By the end of this course I hope to have my short term goals realised so I’m in a position to pursue the longer term ones. While I would like to generate something entirely new for the Devised Project module that might also present an opportunity to begin a new effort on “Trouble’s Brewing”, which I could then continue working on as I enter the workplace. If my efforts to break into the game industry don’t immediately succeed (which is likely given everything that’s going on in the world) I can continue to work on “Trouble’s Brewing” or another project while I work casually, something I’m not actively averse to. That course might also provide me with time to learn Unreal Engine and other valuable technologies.

SMART Goal #1

I want to use a new technology or tool over the course of this module, either as the core development environment for the project or just to improve the quality of the artefact I produce. By the end of the module I want to be at least comfortable with the basics of the tool, but I aim to be confident in my ability to implement it, and in my understanding of its uses.

SMART Goal #2

I feel I have a serious problem with overextending myself on projects, planning too many features and trying to get them all implemented before a deadline. My goal both for this module and going forward is to limit myself to only a few features to begin with. Once these features are complete, properly functioning, and user tested, I will begin to consider additional features, one at a time. I aim to maintain a pattern of controlled iteration.

Concept Outline

Trouble’s Brewing is a game about brewing potions ordered by the citizens of a fantasy town. The player is limited to the interior of the potion shop, and learns about the world outside it through potion orders and from the town crier outside their door.

Trouble’s Brewing was originally worked on by a team of three in a second year undergraduate assignment, however with the blessing of my old teammates I am now working on it independently. The game was intended to be a VR experience, however due to the COVID-19 pandemic I was unable to access the university VR equipment and chose to convert the project into a traditional gameplay experience instead. Despite this I feel VR is still the best way to play Trouble’s Brewing.

In the medium term I would like to develop a full, but small, game entirely on my own, to a higher quality than the prototypes I developed during undergrad. I have a few ideas for this which would probably be very achievable. That said, the game I really want to make is a fully realised version of “Trouble’s Brewing”, the VR game I worked on as part of a team in second year. I think that game is the most promising and interesting idea I’ve worked on.

Commercially Trouble’s Brewing fills a space in the casual games market where narrative exploration is an uncommon element of many games. It also belongs to the high fantasy genre, which is infrequently used in casual games and often to poor effect.

Audience

There are two potential audiences for Trouble’s Brewing, and both benefit from the game’s short gameplay loop.

The first is a casual game audience who want something to play for short periods of time, possibly on a commute. As each “day” in the game lasts approximately 12 minutes it can be experienced in small doses at any time and easily put down when needed. In order to capitalise on this market the game would need to be published on portable devices such as the Nintendo Switch, as the gameplay would not translate well to phones.

The second audience is the VR gaming community. The gameplay of Trouble’s Brewing would be significantly enhanced on VR platforms since the potion making process could be made more tactile. Here again the short gameplay loop is advantageous, as playing in VR can be both physically and mentally tiring after short periods.

Narrative Design

The story told in Trouble’s Brewing takes place entirely outside of the player’s view, however one of the contracts they receive each day is issued by one of the characters who play a central role in the narrative. If the player completes the contract correctly they will see a positive outcome for this character the following day via the Town Crier, but if they don’t complete the contract or do so poorly they will see a negative one.

The player's actions have no impact on the story overall, as the aim is to create a sense of remote helplessness and being unable to do anything but watch. This aim is based primarily on the experience of being stuck at home during the COVID-19 pandemic, but also the general online experience of seeing distant events unfold and being unable to act on them in a meaningful way.

The original direction was to adapt the plot of Naomi Novik’s “Uprooted”, a fantasy-folklore novel, into an interactive experience where the player supports the main characters and hears about the plot through second hand sources. While this concept was ultimately too limiting, I retained the idea of providing narrative context through secondary sources as I felt that was the most appropriate method for the experience I aimed to recreate. This method is used to good effect in “Papers Please”, where the player occasionally reads about the individuals they allow over the border in newspapers later on.

Moving on from this approach I decided to rework the narrative from an original work of mine to fit this game. The first arc of a Dungeons and Dragons campaign I led, “Tales from the Sleepless City”, served very well for the purposes of Trouble’s Brewing, as the narrative had built in points where certain characters had to achieve certain goals, and many of the potions that can be made in Trouble’s Brewing could be used to assist in doing so.

Technologies

Unity was the only logical choice for developing this project, as the first iteration of Trouble’s Brewing was built in Unity. In addition Unity is my strongest development environment, and so to create an appealing product it was best to play to my strengths.

As previously mentioned Trouble’s Brewing was originally a VR game and over the course of developing this non-VR version I began to see that it would play much better on its original platform.

In lieu of being able to develop for VR I decided to deploy Trouble’s Brewing via Unity WebGL, for a couple of reasons. The first is that it makes testing very easy, rather than having to get playtesters to install various versions of the game I am able to update a single version and ask for feedback. The second reason is discoverability, services like itch.io make establishing independently developed games without the support of a publisher much easier, and comes without the costs associated with Steam and other mainstream platforms.
